# Grand Canyon R2R2R Training Plan

**A 24-week, evidence-based program for the Rim-to-Rim-to-Rim**
~42 miles · ~11,000 ft gain / ~11,000 ft loss

---

## How to read this document

This plan is structured in eight parts:

1. **Quick orientation** — your starting point, the gap, and how this plan closes it
2. **Course demands** — what R2R2R actually asks of the body
3. **Training principles** — the science behind every choice in this plan
4. **Tendon rehab framework** — the most important section; everything else depends on this
5. **Phase overview** — the four training blocks and what each one builds
6. **Weekly schedule** — week-by-week sessions for 24 weeks
7. **Supporting programs** — strength, prehab, heat acclimation, fueling
8. **Taper and race day**

A bibliography of primary sources is at the end. The plan is intentionally written so you can read top-to-bottom once, then live in Sections 6–7 day-to-day.

---

## A note on timing and personalization

This plan assumes **24 weeks** to race day. That length is deliberate, not arbitrary:

- You're at ~13 mpw and the event demands ~50+ mpw equivalent training stress at peak
- You have an active tendinopathy that needs gradual, monitored loading
- Aerobic adaptations to low-intensity volume take 12–16 weeks to mature
- Tendon adaptations to loading take 8–12 weeks minimum
- Heat acclimation needs 2–3 weeks at the end

**If you have more than 24 weeks**, extend the base period (Phase 1 and Phase 2). More aerobic base is almost always the right answer when in doubt.

**If you have less than 24 weeks**, the plan does not compress safely given your injury history. The honest options are: (a) push the goal date, (b) accept slower target times and more hiking, or (c) attempt a single-traverse R2R (~24 miles, 5,800 ft gain) instead and target R2R2R the following season.

This plan is a framework. Symptoms in the right ankle override the calendar every time. The single most important rule: **if the tendon talks louder than baseline, listen to it, not to the schedule.**

# Part 1 — Quick Orientation

## Your starting point

- ~12–15 mpw running, 3 sessions/week
- Excellent recent vertical capacity: 40k ft in 4 weeks, peaking at 42k ft in week 4
- Recovering from right posterior tibial tendinopathy (Sept 2025 onset, aggravated by completing a marathon)
- Aerobic and overall endurance under-developed; speed under-developed (lower priority)
- Demonstrated ability to absorb high vertical load over weeks

## What R2R2R demands

- 12–20 hours of continuous moving time
- ~21,000 ft of total elevation change (11,000 up + 11,000 down)
- Sustained eccentric loading on the descents (the part your training mostly hasn't included)
- Aerobic system running near AeT (just below the first lactate breakpoint) for many hours
- Heat exposure at the canyon floor; cold and altitude at the rims
- Self-supported logistics, limited bail-out options

## The gap

Two engines drive R2R2R success: **aerobic engine** (your cardiovascular machinery) and **muscular durability engine** (your legs' ability to keep producing force when damaged, especially on descents).

Your vertical-gain background suggests you have decent local muscular endurance on climbs and a respectable cardiovascular ceiling. Your gap is:

1. **Sustained aerobic capacity at running paces** (not just power-hiking pace)
2. **Tissue durability for repetitive impact** — the loading the marathon aggravated
3. **Eccentric/descent capacity** — the historic destroyer of R2R2R finishers
4. **Tendon health and the strength infrastructure around the medial ankle**

This plan builds those four things while protecting your right tendon.

# Part 2 — Course Demands

## Route assumption

The most common and shortest R2R2R, and the one matching your mileage and vertical numbers, is **South Kaibab → North Kaibab → South Kaibab (SK-NK-SK)**: approximately 42.2 miles, ~10,500–11,000 ft of climbing, ~21,000 ft total elevation change (per Grand Canyon NPS and CTS coaching). The plan assumes this route. If you take Bright Angel one way or both, add ~3–5 miles.

> ⚠️ **2025–2026 closures:** As of late 2025, the North Rim and North Kaibab Trail have been closed due to wildfire damage. The current options when North Rim is closed mean R2R2R is not possible as a true rim-to-rim. Confirm trail status at the NPS Grand Canyon site before committing to a date.

## The four segments

Numbers below are approximate for SK-NK-SK:

| Segment                                               | Miles | Vertical  | Character                                                                          |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ----- | --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. SK descent                                         | ~7    | -4,800 ft | Steep, rocky, sustained eccentric, quad-busting                                    |
| 2. River corridor (Box) → North Kaibab climb to N Rim | ~14   | +5,800 ft | Mild grade across the bottom, then sustained climb; heat exposure low to mid       |
| 3. N Rim descent back to river                        | ~14   | -5,800 ft | Reverse of segment 2; second long sustained eccentric load                         |
| 4. SK climb back to S Rim                             | ~7    | +4,800 ft | The crux. Hottest part of day, on accumulated fatigue, often in the late afternoon |

## What this means for training

1. **Half the day is descent.** Roughly 11,000 ft of it. This is the single biggest reason runners under-perform or DNF. Your eccentric load tolerance must be specifically trained.
2. **The hardest climb comes last.** You need fatigue resistance, not fresh-legged climbing power. Train ME under fatigue.
3. **The canyon inverts mountain logic.** You start the hardest climb when your legs are already cooked and the temperature is highest.
4. **Heat changes everything.** The inner corridor can hit 100°F+ even when the rims are 60°F.
5. **Aerobic threshold pace, not race pace, is the relevant ceiling.** Above your AeT, you cannot sustain effort for 12+ hours.

# Part 3 — Training Principles

This section explains the _why_ behind every workout in this plan. The principles are drawn from the most reputable mountain endurance and running biomechanics sources: Scott Johnston (Evoke Endurance, formerly Uphill Athlete, coach of 2025 UTMB champions Tom Evans and Ruth Croft), Jay Dicharry (_Running Rewired_, _Anatomy for Runners_), Steve House and Kilian Jornet (_Training for the Uphill Athlete_), Jason Koop (_Training Essentials for Ultrarunning_), and peer-reviewed sports medicine literature.

## Principle 1: Train the limiter, not the strength

You have a vertical strength. You have aerobic, durability, and eccentric weaknesses. Most of this plan will not feel "cool" — it will feel slow and unsexy and like you're not training hard enough. That is precisely the point. As Johnston puts it (paraphrased from _Training for the Uphill Athlete_): "doing only what you're good at or what you enjoy" is the most reliable way to underperform.

## Principle 2: The aerobic base is the foundation, and yours needs the most work

For events over ~2 hours, performance is dominated by **aerobic threshold (AeT)** — the highest intensity at which fat oxidation is still dominant and blood lactate hasn't begun to rise meaningfully above baseline. Above AeT, you burn through glycogen faster, accumulate fatigue faster, and cannot sustain effort over canyon-scale timeframes.

Three takeaways for your training:

**(a) Most of your running should be very easy.** Elite mountain athletes commonly spend 75–85% of their training volume below AeT. Recreational athletes consistently train above AeT thinking they're "going easy" and end up in what Johnston calls "no man's land" — too hard to build base, too easy to drive top-end adaptation.

**(b) Test your AeT, don't guess it.** Two practical methods:

- **Nose-breathing test (rough):** Find the fastest pace where you can breathe entirely through your nose. That pace is approximately your AeT.
- **Heart-rate drift test (better):** On a flat 1-hour run at a constant comfortable effort, your heart rate should rise by **less than 5%** from the first half to the second half (pace-corrected). If it rises more, you started above AeT. (Upload to a service like TrainingPeaks Premium and look at Pa:Hr; <5% drift = at or below AeT.) Re-test every 6–8 weeks.
- **Backup formula (the Maffetone/Phil Maffetone 180-age method, used by Uphill Athlete as a conservative starting point):** Estimated AeT ≈ 180 − age (subtract another 5 if returning from injury or sickness, which applies to you). Use this only until you've done a drift test.

**(c) The 10% spread test.** When the difference between your AeT and your lactate threshold (LT) shrinks to ≤10% of your HR, you've built a strong aerobic base and can shift emphasis to higher-intensity event-specific work. If the gap is wider, keep building base. Most runners coming back from inactivity or injury have wide spreads — they're "aerobically deficient" — and benefit dramatically from disciplined low-intensity work.

## Principle 3: Polarized intensity distribution

The training-intensity research (Stephen Seiler and colleagues, replicated across endurance sports) is unusually consistent: world-class endurance athletes spend roughly **80% of their training time below AeT (Zone 2 or lower), and the remaining 15–20% in higher-intensity work**, with very little time in the middle "tempo" zone. This pattern beats more time spent at moderate intensity in nearly every controlled comparison.

Four-zone framework used throughout this plan:

| Zone                             | Definition        | Effort                                                    | Use in this plan                                 |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Z1 (recovery)**                | <90% of AeT HR    | Conversational, restorative                               | Recovery days, warmups                           |
| **Z2 (aerobic base)**            | 90–100% of AeT HR | Comfortable conversation, full sentences, nose-breathable | The bulk of your running                         |
| **Z3 (tempo / "no man's land")** | AeT to LT         | Choppy sentences, "comfortably hard"                      | Used sparingly — sub-threshold long efforts only |
| **Z4 (threshold and above)**     | ≥LT               | Short answers, hard breathing                             | Hill repeats, intervals in late phases           |

**Z3 trap:** This is where runners ego-pace their easy runs. It's too hard to maintain volume safely and not hard enough to drive top-end adaptation. Most days, you should be either Z2 (easy) or Z4 (genuinely hard). Z3 is a deliberate, occasional tool — not a default.

## Principle 4: Muscular Endurance (ME) is the canyon's true currency

Scott Johnston's work with elite ultra athletes (Tom Evans, Killian Jornet, Ruth Croft) increasingly emphasizes that for long mountain events, **local muscular endurance — the ability of leg muscles to keep producing force when the aerobic engine still has plenty in the tank — is the limiting factor late in the race**. Your aerobic machinery can be world-class and you'll still get crushed by quads that have failed.

ME work for R2R2R involves:

- Steep uphill carries with a moderately weighted pack (15–25% of body weight)
- Long downhill repeats focused on form, not speed
- High-rep loaded step-ups and lunges
- Long climbing repeats at sub-AeT effort

ME work should **stress the legs locally without driving heart rate high enough to systemic fatigue you**. The cue: your legs feel worked, but your breathing is comfortable.

## Principle 5: Strength training is non-negotiable

The strongest evidence in the running performance literature supports **2x/week heavy strength training** for distance runners. A 2024 meta-analysis (Llanos-Lagos et al., _Sports Medicine_) of 38 studies found that **strength training using heavy loads (≥80% 1RM) improves time trial and time-to-exhaustion performance**, and combined methods (heavy + plyometric) produced the largest effects. Blagrove's 2018 systematic review and his 2025 RCT (Zanini et al., _Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise_) showed strength training improves **running economy durability** — exactly the trait that matters at hour 10 of an ultra.

This plan uses two strength sessions per week throughout, with the focus shifting from corrective/foundation → max strength → muscular endurance across the phases. Detail in Part 7.

## Principle 6: Movement quality matters as much as load (the Running Rewired insight)

Jay Dicharry's central thesis in _Running Rewired_ is that strength training without movement quality just trains compensations. For a runner returning from posterior tibial tendinopathy, this is especially important — the tendon was overloaded partly because something upstream wasn't doing its share. Common culprits:

- Weak gluteus medius → hip drop → tibia rotation → medial ankle overload
- Stiff big toe / poor toe-off mechanics → arch collapse → tib post overload
- Foot intrinsic weakness → arch collapse → tib post overload
- Poor single-leg stability → repeated micro-correction loads on the tendon

The prehab/mobility routine in Part 7 specifically targets these.

## Principle 7: Tendons need progressive load, not rest

Modern tendinopathy management (Cook & Purdam's continuum model; Silbernagel, Beyer, Kongsgaard, Alfredson protocols) is unambiguous: **rest does not heal a degenerative tendon.** Progressive mechanical loading — gradually increasing, with adequate recovery between bouts — drives tendon remodeling. The right approach for your tendon is:

1. **Reduce aggravating load** (running mileage) only as much as needed to keep symptoms manageable
2. **Apply loaded exercise**, primarily heavy-slow resistance (HSR), 3x/week with 48+ hours between sessions
3. **Monitor symptoms** with the 24-hour rule (see Part 4)
4. **Progress slowly but consistently** for at least 12 weeks

The current best evidence (Beyer 2015, Malliaras 2013, multiple systematic reviews) suggests **HSR is at least as effective as eccentric-only protocols and has better adherence**. This plan uses HSR as the primary loading modality.

## Principle 8: Eccentric/downhill training has powerful protective effects

The "repeated bout effect" is one of the most robust findings in exercise physiology: a single bout of eccentric exercise provides substantial protection against muscle damage from subsequent eccentric bouts, with the protection lasting weeks. For descents, this means **you must train descents specifically and progressively**, not just hope your climbing fitness translates.

Without specific downhill work, you'll get to mile 25 of R2R2R with quads that have never absorbed that magnitude of eccentric load and they will fail. With even modest downhill-specific training built into the plan, the damage at race pace is significantly attenuated.

## Principle 9: Heat acclimation is a free performance gain

Even 6–7 consecutive days of heat exposure (≥30 min, achieving core temp ~38.5°C, by exercise-in-heat or post-exercise sauna) produces measurable plasma volume expansion, improved sweat response, lower exercise heart rate, and improved performance in both hot AND temperate conditions. The bulk of adaptation comes in 10–14 days. This plan includes a 14-day heat block in the final 2.5 weeks before race day.

## Principle 10: Conservative progression, with recovery cycles

The single best predictor of running-related injury is sudden load spikes. The literature is messier than the popular "10% rule" suggests — some studies find <20%/week increases are safe; competitive runners and the Comrades ultramarathon cohort showed _low_ training loads were the bigger risk for injury during the race. The synthesis:

- **Build in 3-week-on, 1-week-off cycles** (a "deload" or recovery week at ~60–70% of the prior week's volume)
- **Single-week increases generally stay ≤15%**, but the principle is bigger than the number: track your subjective fatigue, sleep, and morning resting HR alongside volume
- **Never increase volume and intensity simultaneously** in the same week
- **Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR)** between 0.8 and 1.3 is the safer zone; >1.5 spikes risk

This plan uses 3:1 build:recovery throughout.

# Part 4 — Tendon Rehab Framework (Posterior Tibial Tendinopathy)

**Read this before any other section of the plan.** Your right tendon is the gating factor for everything else.

## Step zero: Get a current clinical assessment

If you have not seen a sports-focused PT in the last 3 months, **see one before starting this plan**. They will:

- Confirm the diagnosis (tibialis posterior tendinopathy vs. tendon dysfunction with progressive collapsing flatfoot; vs. tibialis posterior insertional vs. mid-portion vs. dysfunction stage II)
- Assess single-leg heel raise capacity (the most reliable functional diagnostic)
- Identify upstream contributors (hip control, big toe mobility, foot intrinsic strength)
- Determine if you should use an orthotic, medial post, rocker shoe, or none

Nothing in this document substitutes for that assessment.

## How tendons heal: the load-pain-response framework

The Cook & Purdam continuum model: tendons exist along a spectrum from _normal_ → _reactive_ → _disrepair_ → _degenerative_. Tendons heal by being **loaded in a way that's just below the threshold that worsens symptoms**, repeatedly, over months. They do not heal by being rested.

**The 24-hour rule** (the standard for tendinopathy rehab in the sports medicine literature):

- Symptoms during loading at ≤3/10 pain are acceptable
- Symptoms within 24 hours after loading should return to baseline (your starting morning pain)
- If morning pain the day after a session is higher than baseline → that session was too much; back off the load by 20–30% next time
- If morning pain stays elevated for 24+ hours → drop further and reassess

You're aiming for: discomfort during loading, no flare in the 24 hours after.

## The four phases of tendon loading

The plan structures tendon work in four phases. These overlap with the training phases but are tracked separately.

### Phase A — Isometric calming (Weeks 1–3 if symptomatic; skip if asymptomatic)

If your tendon is currently painful with daily activity, start here. Isometric loading reduces pain in many tendinopathies (Rio et al. 2015 for patellar tendons; less clear for tib post but the mechanism likely transfers).

- **Heavy isometric calf hold, knee straight:** 5 reps × 30–45 seconds, holding a heavy hold (single-leg if possible, otherwise weighted), 80%+ max effort. 1–2x daily.
- **Heavy isometric calf hold, knee bent (soleus focus):** same dosing.
- **Optional, more tib-post-specific:** Resisted inversion isometric (band around forefoot, foot turned inward) — 5 × 30 sec.

Pain during hold up to 3/10 is fine. Pain >3/10 → reduce load.

### Phase B — Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) (begin once symptoms allow daily living without flare, continues through Weeks 4–14)

This is the workhorse phase, supported by the strongest evidence (Beyer 2015; Malliaras 2013; multiple Wexner clinical practice guidelines). HSR involves slow, heavy, controlled tempo loading with both concentric and eccentric phases.

**Tempo:** 3 seconds up, 3 seconds down (some protocols use 3-1-3 or 3-3 with no pause).

**Frequency:** 3x/week, with at least 48 hours between sessions.

**Loading:** Start at ~12RM (i.e., a load you could do for 12 reps to failure). Each session, perform 3–4 sets of 12 reps. Every 2 weeks, increase the load and reduce the rep range slightly (12RM → 10RM → 8RM → 6RM). By weeks 12–14, you should be loading at near-6RM with full pain-free range.

**Core HSR exercises (do all three each session):**

1. **Bilateral heel raise on a step**, with a slow lower into dorsiflexion. Add weight (dumbbells, weighted pack, or weighted vest) as you progress. Knee straight.
2. **Bilateral heel raise on a step, knee bent ~30°** (soleus emphasis). Same loading principle.
3. **Single-leg heel raise on a step** (once cleared to do this without pain). Start at body weight, progress to weighted. This is the most tib-post-specific movement.

**Adjunct HSR exercises (rotate 1–2 per session):**

4. **Resisted ankle inversion** (band): 3 × 15 slow reps, building to 3 × 8 with heavier band
5. **Foot doming / short foot exercise** (Dicharry / Janda) — 2 × 30 with hold; this trains foot intrinsics, especially tibialis posterior's partners

### Phase C — Elastic / functional loading (Weeks 12–18)

Once HSR loads are tolerated comfortably at ~8RM, you add elastic/SSC (stretch-shortening cycle) work to prepare the tendon for running's impact loading.

Add weekly:

- **Pogo hops** (in place, soft landing): 3 × 20, focus on minimal contact time
- **Single-leg pogo hops** (once bilateral is easy and pain-free): 3 × 15 each leg
- **Skipping for height** (A-skips, B-skips): 3 × 30 m
- **Low-amplitude lateral hops** (to challenge inversion): 3 × 15 each direction

Pain rule: same 24-hour rule. If a session causes a next-morning flare, regress to HSR-only for a week.

### Phase D — Sport-specific / maintenance (Weeks 18+)

Once HSR is heavy (6RM) and elastic work is tolerated, the explicit tendon work transitions to maintenance — usually 1x/week of single-leg HSR plus the elastic drills as part of warmup. The bulk of the loading stimulus by this point is your actual running and strength work.

## Running modifications to protect the tendon

While the tendon is rebuilding capacity, three small running technique cues (drawn from Dicharry, Brindle 2014, and the Maharaj 2019 walking-width study) can reduce tibialis posterior demand:

1. **Slightly wider stance width.** If you cross over (one foot landing in front of the midline of the other), widen your stance about a half-shoe width. Imagine running on train tracks, not a tightrope.
2. **Cadence ~5% higher than your default.** Shorter, quicker steps reduce peak loads. If you naturally run at 165 spm, aim for 170–175.
3. **Run on softer, smoother surfaces when possible** during the rebuild — packed trail, treadmill, dirt road, track. Avoid steep cambered roads and gnarly off-camber technical trail until late phases.

## When to back off

Stop or modify the session immediately and consult your PT if:

- Sharp pain (vs. dull ache) along the medial ankle/foot
- Pain that makes you alter your gait
- Swelling that doesn't resolve overnight
- A new "popping" or "snapping" sensation
- Loss of single-leg heel raise capacity

These are signs of progression to more serious tendon pathology and warrant imaging.

# Part 5 — Phase Overview

The 24 weeks divide into four phases. Each block targets a different limiter, but they overlap — you don't suddenly stop doing aerobic base in Phase 3; you just shift emphasis.

| Phase                  | Weeks | Primary focus                                                | Weekly volume target  | Vertical target     |
| ---------------------- | ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------- | ------------------- |
| **1 — Reintroduction** | 1–6   | Tendon rehab, movement quality, easy aerobic                 | 15 → 25 mi            | 1,500 → 3,000 ft    |
| **2 — Base**           | 7–12  | Aerobic capacity, strength foundation, downhill introduction | 25 → 38 mi            | 3,000 → 6,000 ft    |
| **3 — Specific Build** | 13–18 | Muscular endurance, long efforts, heat tolerance             | 35 → 50 mi            | 6,000 → 10,000 ft   |
| **4 — Peak & Taper**   | 19–24 | Race-specific simulation, heat acclim, taper                 | Peak 55 → taper to 25 | Peak 12,000 → taper |

Each phase ends with a recovery week (~60% of prior week's volume).

## What each phase looks like at a glance

### Phase 1 — Reintroduction (Weeks 1–6)

The goal is **not** to build fitness. The goal is to rebuild the infrastructure that lets you build fitness. Most of the work is tendon loading and movement quality. Running is all Z2, kept short. By week 6 you should be running 4x/week comfortably with no tendon flare.

### Phase 2 — Base (Weeks 7–12)

Aerobic volume increases meaningfully. Two key sessions per week emerge: a long Z2 run on the weekend, and a mid-week Z2 run with introductory hill repeats or downhill cruising. Strength work shifts to heavy compound lifts.

### Phase 3 — Specific Build (Weeks 13–18)

The plan begins to look like R2R2R. Long back-to-back weekends. Muscular endurance work with weighted vest/pack. First descent-focused long efforts. Trekking poles introduced. Long climbs at sub-AeT effort.

### Phase 4 — Peak & Taper (Weeks 19–24)

Two peak weeks (19, 21) bracketing one recovery week (20). The biggest single days. Heat acclimation block runs through the last 2.5 weeks. The final 10 days are taper.

# Part 7 — Supporting Programs

## A. Strength Training Program

Two 45–60 min sessions per week, separated by at least 48 hours. The structure shifts across phases. **Always preceded by 5 min of the dynamic warm-up (Section B) and the prehab routine (Section C).**

### Block 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–6) — Tissue prep, movement quality

The point is to groove patterns and tolerate basic loading. Loads light to moderate.

**Strength A — Lower body / posterior chain**

1. Goblet squat: 3 × 8 (moderate)
2. Romanian deadlift: 3 × 8 (moderate)
3. **Bulgarian split squat: 3 × 8 each leg** (bodyweight if needed first 2 weeks, then load)
4. **Single-leg glute bridge: 3 × 12 each side**
5. Side plank: 3 × 30 sec each side
6. **HSR calf circuit** (see Part 4, Phase B)

**Strength B — Lower body / anterior chain / core**

1. Reverse lunge: 3 × 8 each leg (bodyweight, then load)
2. **Step-up (knee-height box), driving through full hip extension: 3 × 8 each leg**
3. Dead bug: 3 × 10 each side
4. Pallof press (anti-rotation): 3 × 10 each side
5. **Single-leg balance series (eyes closed, then with ball toss): 3 × 30 sec each leg**
6. **HSR calf circuit**

### Block 2: Max Strength (Weeks 7–12) — Drive force production

The block where heavy compound lifts produce the running-economy benefits documented in Blagrove's work. Loads heavy (~80–85% 1RM by week 12). Lower reps, longer rest, controlled tempo.

**Strength A**

1. Back squat or goblet squat: 4 × 5 (build to 80% 1RM)
2. Romanian deadlift: 4 × 5 (heavy)
3. **Bulgarian split squat, weighted: 3 × 6 each leg**
4. **Single-leg deadlift: 3 × 6 each leg**
5. Side plank with hip abduction: 3 × 10 each side
6. HSR calf

**Strength B**

1. Front squat or trap-bar deadlift: 4 × 5
2. **Weighted step-up: 4 × 5 each leg, drive through hip extension**
3. **Single-leg calf raise off step, weighted: 4 × 8 each side** (this is also tib post work)
4. Cable pull-through or kettlebell swing: 3 × 8
5. Hollow-body hold: 3 × 30 sec
6. **Foot intrinsic work: toe yoga, doming, short foot — 5 min**

**Add plyometric primer at end of one session per week:**

- Pogo hops 3 × 20
- Box jumps (low box, soft landing) 3 × 6
- Skipping for height 3 × 30m

### Block 3: Muscular Endurance (Weeks 13–18) — Specific to canyon demands

Reps go up, loads stay moderate-heavy, tempo is controlled. Sessions feel like the strength version of an ultra: long, sustained, eventually grinding.

**Strength A — Heavy compound + ME circuit**

1. Back squat: 3 × 6 at 75% 1RM (maintenance heavy)
2. Romanian deadlift: 3 × 6
3. **ME circuit, 3 rounds with 90 sec between rounds:**
   - Walking lunge × 20 steps (loaded with 15–20 lb)
   - Step-up onto knee-height box × 12 each leg
   - SL calf raise × 15 each side
   - Plank with reach × 10 each side

**Strength B — Single-leg ME emphasis**

1. Front squat: 3 × 6
2. Trap bar deadlift: 3 × 6
3. **ME circuit, 3 rounds:**
   - **Bulgarian split squat × 12 each leg, weighted**
   - **Backward lunge × 12 each leg, weighted** (specifically protects quads for downhill)
   - **SL Romanian deadlift × 10 each leg**
   - SL pogo hops × 15 each leg
4. Hollow body or side plank rotations

### Block 4: Maintenance (Weeks 19–24)

Reduce volume by ~30%; preserve neural patterns. Drop heaviest sets, keep one heavy and one ME session weekly. By week 23, sessions are 30 minutes, light, movement-quality focused. Week 24: no strength.

## B. Dynamic Warm-up (5–10 min, every run and every strength session)

Inspired by the Running Rewired warm-ups and FIFA 11+ research-supported sequence.

1. **Hip circles** — 10 each direction, each leg
2. **Leg swings** (forward/back, side/side) — 10 each direction, each leg
3. **Walking lunges with rotation** — 10 total
4. **Lateral lunges** — 8 each side
5. **A-skips** — 30 m
6. **B-skips** — 30 m
7. **High knees** — 30 m
8. **Butt kicks** — 30 m
9. **Cariocas** — 30 m each direction
10. **4 × 20 sec strides** — gradually accelerate, focus on form

## C. Prehab / Mobility Routine (10–15 min daily — non-negotiable)

The ankle-foot-hip routine that protects the tibialis posterior. Drawn from Dicharry's _Running Rewired_, E3 Rehab's tibialis posterior protocol, and standard PT practice.

**Daily (especially mornings or before runs):**

1. **Ankle alphabet** — write the alphabet with the foot, each leg. 1 round.
2. **Toe yoga** — lift big toe with other toes down, then reverse. 3 × 10 each foot.
3. **Foot doming / short foot** — sit, foot flat, draw the ball of the foot toward the heel without curling toes. Hold 5 sec × 10 reps.
4. **Marble pickups or towel scrunches** — 1 minute each foot.
5. **Single-leg balance** — 30 sec each leg, then 30 sec with eyes closed. Progress to unstable surface.
6. **Wall ankle dorsiflexion mobilization** — knee toward wall over toe, 10 reps each side.
7. **Calf stretch (knee straight)** — 2 × 30 sec each side.
8. **Calf stretch (knee bent, soleus)** — 2 × 30 sec each side.
9. **Hip flexor stretch** (couch stretch) — 2 × 30 sec each side.
10. **Glute medius activation** — clam shells with band, 2 × 15 each side. Side-lying hip abduction, 2 × 12 each side.

**Post-run additions:**

- Foam roll quads, IT band, calves, glutes — 1–2 minutes per muscle group.
- Light walking 5 min as cool-down.

## D. Heat Acclimation Protocol

Begin Week 17 (8 weeks before race day), continue through race week.

**Why these methods:** Passive post-exercise heat exposure (sauna or hot bath) produces the same plasma volume expansion, lower exercise HR, and improved heat tolerance as exercise-in-heat protocols, with fewer drawbacks. The Zurawlew (2016), Scoon (2007), and broader literature show meaningful adaptations from 6–7 sessions, with greater adaptation from 10–14 sessions.

**Protocol options (pick one and stick with it):**

**Option 1 — Sauna (most studied)**

- 4 sessions/week, immediately post-exercise (or within 30 min)
- Traditional sauna: 80–90°C / 175–195°F
- Duration: start 15 min, progress by 2–3 min/session to 30 min by week 19
- Target: pleasantly hot, sweating heavily, but able to nose-breathe. If dizzy or nauseous, exit immediately
- Hydrate 0.5 L water with electrolytes before, 0.5–1 L after

**Option 2 — Hot bath**

- 4 sessions/week, post-exercise
- Water temperature: ~40°C / 104°F
- Submerge to shoulders, 30–40 min per session
- Same hydration protocol

**Option 3 — Outdoor heat run (if you live somewhere warm enough)**

- 1–2 runs/week in midday heat, easy Z1–Z2 effort, 40–60 min
- More than this risks training stress + heat stress overlap → injury

**During heat block:**

- Don't combine with hard training days; do sauna _after_ hard sessions when possible
- Weigh yourself before and after to track fluid loss; replace 125–150% of fluid lost over the following 4 hours
- Monitor morning resting HR; a sustained 5+ bpm elevation = back off

## E. Trekking Pole Training

Trekking poles meaningfully reduce leg muscle load on long descents (multiple studies) and improve climbing economy by ~5%. They're standard equipment for R2R2R. **Don't use them for the first time on race day.**

Introduce in Week 9. Use on at least two long runs per week starting Week 13. Practice:

- Setting them up quickly (race day will be cold)
- Stowing them for runnable sections
- Pole plant timing on steep ups (push hard with arms, every other step rhythm)
- Using them defensively on steep downs (in front, light touch, to brake/balance, not bear weight)

Recommended: collapsible carbon poles, 105–125 cm depending on height; the lightest setup you can afford to carry.

## F. Fueling Strategy

R2R2R is a 12–20 hour event. Bonking and GI failure are the most common in-race issues after quad failure. The protocol below is built around current sports nutrition consensus (Burke, Stellingwerff, Jeukendrup) for ultra-endurance fueling, with R2R2R-specific adjustments.

**Training fueling targets (long runs >2 hours):**

- **60–80 g carbohydrate per hour** for shorter long runs (2–4 hr)
- **80–100 g carbohydrate per hour** for runs over 4 hours, achievable using glucose+fructose blend products (gels, drink mixes, food)
- Practice EVERY long run from Week 9 onward. Train the gut like you train the legs.
- 500–750 mL fluid per hour, more in heat
- 500–1000 mg sodium per hour, more if you're a salty sweater

**Race-day fueling target:**

- **80–100 g carbs per hour** (you'll want carb stores high before, low risk of bonk)
- Variety: gels, drink mix, real food (PB+honey wraps, dates, salted potatoes, gummy candies — whatever your gut tolerates)
- Real food becomes more important after hour 6 when gel fatigue sets in

**Sweat rate test (do Weeks 14, 17, 20):**

- Empty bladder, weigh nude
- Run 60 minutes at Z2, drink known amount of fluid
- Weigh nude again
- Sweat rate (L/hour) = body weight loss (kg) + fluid drunk (L) ÷ duration (hr)
- Multiply by 1.1 for the canyon heat estimate

**Carb-loading:** 3 days out, target 8–10 g carbs per kg bodyweight per day. The classic taper carb-load. Avoid fiber and fat overload the day before; eat plain, familiar food.

## G. Recovery & Monitoring

Track these daily in a simple notebook or app:

1. **Morning resting HR.** Persistent 5+ bpm elevation = fatigue accumulating; needs an extra rest day.
2. **Subjective energy (1–10).** Below 6 for 3+ days = back off.
3. **Right ankle pain (0–10 at first weight-bearing step).** Anything >2 trending upward = scale back the next planned session.
4. **Sleep duration and quality.** Sleep is the single biggest recovery lever; protect it.
5. **Mood and motivation.** If runs are dreaded for 5+ days straight, you're underrecovered.

**The four-rule traffic light:**

| If…                                                  | Then                                                |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Tendon at baseline, energy good, sleep good          | Proceed with planned session                        |
| Tendon mildly above baseline (≤1/10), energy reduced | Reduce planned session by 25%, drop intensity       |
| Tendon symptomatic (2–4/10), or 2+ poor sleep nights | Skip the hard session, replace with easy Z1 or rest |
| Tendon painful (>4/10) or any sharp pain             | Stop. Re-read Part 4. See PT before next session.   |

# Part 8 — Taper, Race Day, and Logistics

## Taper logic

The taper literature is consistent: ~2 weeks of reduced volume (40–60% drop in total volume over the taper period), maintained or slightly increased _relative_ intensity (i.e., keep doing strides and short hill repeats), preserves and often improves fitness. Most performance gains from a good taper come from glycogen restoration, tendon repair, and small nervous-system adaptations.

The Week 23–24 schedule above implements this. **Resist the urge to "test" your fitness with extra hard workouts in the final two weeks.** Every extra hard effort risks injury or fatigue with zero upside.

## Race-day pacing strategy

The course has four distinct sections (see Part 2). The pacing principle: **conserve aggressively in segments 1 and 2; finish strong in segments 3 and 4.**

**Segment 1 — SK descent (~7 mi, -4,800 ft, target time: 1:15–1:45 hr)**

- Slowest sustainable downhill technique. Quad savings here = quad reserves at the end.
- Heart rate well below AeT — this should feel like a warmup.
- Trekking poles deployed.
- Fuel: 80 g carbs, 0.5 L fluid.

**Segment 2 — Box + N Rim climb (~14 mi, +5,800 ft, target time: 4–5 hr)**

- Steady Z2 effort. Run flats and gentle ups; hike anything over ~8% grade.
- Fuel: 80–100 g carbs per hour, 0.5–0.75 L per hour
- This is the long, sustained section where rhythm and patience matter. Don't surge.
- At N Rim turnaround: top off fluids, eat real food, briefly stretch, restart.

**Segment 3 — N Rim descent back to river (~14 mi, -5,800 ft, target time: 2:30–3:30 hr)**

- The most dangerous segment. Quads are now warmed up but not yet destroyed. Tempting to fly down.
- Don't. Cruise. Use poles defensively. Land with bent knees, short stride.
- Heart rate moderate (Z2). Effort should feel relaxed even though pace is fast.
- Fuel: same as before. By now eating may feel harder; force it.

**Segment 4 — SK climb back to S Rim (~7 mi, +4,800 ft, target time: 2:30–3:30 hr)**

- The crux. Hottest, hardest, most fatigued.
- Hike all of it except briefly runnable flat sections at trailheads.
- Pole work matters here. Plant aggressively, push with arms.
- Fuel: continue 80 g/hour even if appetite is gone. Drop carbs from gels to whatever you can stomach — fruit, soda, salted nuts, real food.
- Manage heat aggressively: dunk shirt and hat at every water source, eat ice if available, slow down before overheating not after.
- Mantra: short steps, steady breathing, don't stop unless you need to.

## Heat management

- Start before sunrise — ideally 3–5 AM if temperatures will exceed 80°F at the bottom by midday.
- Goal: be on the climb up SK before noon if possible.
- Carry a buff or light cap that can be soaked.
- Dunk every shirt/hat opportunity in every water source.
- If you feel chills, stop sweating, or get nauseated → heat illness warning, stop and cool down.

## Mandatory gear (R2R2R-specific)

- Pack with 1.5–2 L hydration capacity
- 2x soft flasks (refill flexibility)
- Water filter or purification tablets (the canyon water system is unreliable; tap closures happen mid-day)
- Trekking poles
- Headlamp + spare batteries (you will likely be on the trail in pre-dawn dark)
- Light wind shell (for rim cold)
- Light layer (the rims are 20–30°F cooler than the bottom)
- Trail running gaiters (sand and grit destroy feet)
- Emergency blanket / bivy
- First-aid kit (blister supplies, anti-chafe, ibuprofen)
- Printed trail map (no cell service)
- 2,500+ calories of food
- 1,500+ mg sodium beyond what's in gels/drink mix
- ID, parking pass, emergency contact card

## What to do in the last 48 hours

**Friday (race-1):**

- Travel to South Rim, arrive by early afternoon
- Drive the trailhead access; identify parking
- Walk 20–30 min easy at the rim to acclimate to altitude
- Eat dinner by 6 PM — familiar, moderate-carb, low-fat, low-fiber
- Lay out all gear, pack, recheck list twice
- Bed by 8 PM. Set alarm for 2:30–3 AM.

**Race morning:**

- Wake 90+ min before start
- Eat 100–150 g carbs (oatmeal, banana, toast, sports drink)
- Coffee if normal for you
- Don't try anything new
- At trailhead: 30 g carb gel + 250 mL water 15 min before starting
- Cold rim — warm layer on, knowing you'll strip in the first mile

**The day after:**

- Eat. Sleep. Walk only.
- Foam-roll lightly.
- Within a week: 30-min easy walk, then a 20-min jog if pain-free.
- Do not return to structured training for at least 2 weeks. The eccentric damage is bigger than you think.
