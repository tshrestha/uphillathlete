01 Quick Orientation

Where you are, where the canyon is, and how this plan closes the gap.

This plan assumes 24 weeks to race day. That length isn't arbitrary — it reflects what the body actually needs: 12–16 weeks for aerobic adaptations to mature, 8–12 weeks minimum for tendon remodeling, and 2–3 weeks for heat acclimation at the end. If you have more time, extend the base period. If you have less, the honest answer is to push the date or downgrade to a single R2R.

Your starting point

  • ~12–15 mpw running, 3 sessions/week
  • Excellent recent vertical capacity: 40k ft in 4 weeks, peaking at 42k ft in week 4
  • Recovering from right posterior tibial tendinopathy (Sept 2025, aggravated by completing a marathon)
  • Aerobic and overall endurance under-developed; speed under-developed (lower priority)

What R2R2R demands

  • 12–20 hours of continuous moving time
  • ~21,000 ft of total elevation change (11,000 up + 11,000 down)
  • Sustained eccentric loading on the descents — the part your training mostly hasn't included
  • Aerobic system running near AeT for many hours
  • Heat exposure at the canyon floor; cold and altitude at the rims
  • Self-supported logistics, limited bail-out options

The gap

Two engines drive R2R2R success: the aerobic engine (cardiovascular machinery) and the muscular durability engine (legs' ability to keep producing force when damaged). Your vertical-gain background suggests decent local muscular endurance on climbs and a respectable cardiovascular ceiling. Your gap is fourfold:

  1. Sustained aerobic capacity at running paces, not just power-hiking pace
  2. Tissue durability for repetitive impact — the loading the marathon aggravated
  3. Eccentric / descent capacity — the historic destroyer of R2R2R finishers
  4. Tendon health and the strength infrastructure around the medial ankle

This plan builds those four things while protecting your right tendon.

The two rules that override everything

1. The tendon's voice wins. Every time. The training schedule is a suggestion; the tendon's response is data.

2. Easy days are easy. Hard days are hard. Nothing lives in the middle.

02 Course Demands

The canyon, segment by segment.

The most common and shortest R2R2R, and the one matching the standard ~42 mi / 11,000 ft figures, is South Kaibab → North Kaibab → South Kaibab. The plan assumes this route. If you take Bright Angel either direction, add 3–5 miles.

⚠ 2025–26 Closures

As of late 2025, the North Rim and North Kaibab Trail are closed due to wildfire damage. When the North Rim is closed, R2R2R as a true rim-to-rim is not possible. Confirm trail status at the NPS Grand Canyon site before committing to a date.

The four segments

Segment Miles Vertical Character
1. SK descent ~7 −4,800 ft Steep, rocky, sustained eccentric, quad-busting
2. River corridor → N Rim climb ~14 +5,800 ft Mild grade across the bottom, then sustained climb
3. N Rim descent back to river ~14 −5,800 ft Reverse of segment 2; second long sustained eccentric load
4. SK climb back to S Rim ~7 +4,800 ft The crux. Hottest part of day, on accumulated fatigue

What this means for training

  1. Half the day is descent. ~11,000 ft of it. This is the single biggest reason runners under-perform or DNF. Eccentric load tolerance must be specifically trained.
  2. The hardest climb comes last. You need fatigue resistance, not fresh-legged climbing power.
  3. The canyon inverts mountain logic. You start the hardest climb when your legs are already cooked and the temperature is highest.
  4. Heat changes everything. The inner corridor can hit 100°F+ even when the rims are 60°F.
  5. Aerobic threshold pace, not race pace, is the relevant ceiling. Above AeT, you cannot sustain effort for 12+ hours.
03 Training Principles

Ten ideas that drive every workout in this plan.

Drawn from Scott Johnston (Evoke Endurance, coach of 2025 UTMB champions Tom Evans & Ruth Croft), Jay Dicharry (Running Rewired), House & Jornet (Training for the Uphill Athlete), Jason Koop, and the peer-reviewed sports medicine literature.

1

Train the limiter, not the strength.

You have a vertical strength. You have aerobic, durability, and eccentric weaknesses. Most of this plan will not feel "cool." That's the point. Johnston: doing only what you're good at is the most reliable way to underperform.

2

The aerobic base is the foundation, and yours needs the most work.

For events over ~2 hours, performance is dominated by aerobic threshold (AeT) — the intensity where fat oxidation still dominates. Three takeaways:

  • Most running should be very easy. Elites spend 75–85% below AeT.
  • Test your AeT, don't guess it. Nose-breathing pace works as a rough guide; a 60-minute heart-rate drift test (HR rise <5% at constant pace) is better.
  • The 10% spread test. When AeT and lactate threshold (LT) are within 10% of each other, you've built a strong base. Most returning-from-injury runners have wide spreads.
3

Polarized intensity distribution.

~80% of training below AeT, ~15–20% high intensity, very little in the middle. This pattern (Seiler and colleagues, replicated across endurance sports) beats more time at moderate intensity in nearly every controlled comparison. Z3 is the "no man's land" trap: too hard to maintain volume safely, not hard enough to drive top-end adaptation. Most days: Z2 or Z4. Not in between.

4

Muscular Endurance is the canyon's true currency.

Johnston's recent work with elite ultra athletes emphasizes that for long mountain events, local muscular endurance — legs producing force when the aerobic engine still has plenty in the tank — is the limiting factor late in the race. ME work stresses legs locally without driving HR high enough for systemic fatigue. The cue: legs feel worked, breathing comfortable.

5

Strength training is non-negotiable.

The 2024 Llanos-Lagos meta-analysis (38 studies): heavy strength training (≥80% 1RM) improves time trial and TTE performance. Blagrove's 2025 RCT showed strength training improves running economy durability — exactly the trait that matters at hour 10 of an ultra. Two sessions per week, throughout.

6

Movement quality matters as much as load.

Jay Dicharry's central thesis: strength training without movement quality just trains compensations. For a runner returning from posterior tibial tendinopathy, common upstream culprits include weak gluteus medius (→ hip drop → tibial rotation → medial ankle overload), stiff big toe, foot intrinsic weakness, and poor single-leg stability. The prehab routine targets these specifically.

7

Tendons need progressive load, not rest.

Modern tendinopathy management (Cook & Purdam continuum; Silbernagel, Beyer, Kongsgaard, Alfredson protocols) is unambiguous: rest does not heal a degenerative tendon. Progressive mechanical loading — gradually increasing, with adequate recovery — drives remodeling. Heavy-slow resistance (HSR) is the primary modality; current best evidence shows it's at least as effective as eccentric-only protocols, with better adherence.

8

Eccentric/downhill training has powerful protective effects.

The "repeated bout effect" is one of the most robust findings in exercise physiology: a single bout of eccentric exercise provides substantial protection against damage from subsequent bouts, lasting weeks. Without specific downhill work, you'll get to mile 25 with quads that have never absorbed that magnitude of eccentric load — and they will fail.

9

Heat acclimation is a free performance gain.

Even 6–7 consecutive days of heat exposure (≥30 min, core temp ~38.5°C, via exercise-in-heat or post-exercise sauna) produces measurable plasma volume expansion, lower exercise HR, and improved performance in both hot and temperate conditions. The plan includes a 14-day heat block in the final 2.5 weeks.

10

Conservative progression, with recovery cycles.

The single best predictor of running injury is sudden load spikes. The plan uses 3-weeks-on, 1-week-off cycles with recovery weeks at ~60–70% of prior week's volume. Single-week increases stay ≤15%. Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio between 0.8 and 1.3 is the safer zone; >1.5 spikes risk. Never increase volume and intensity simultaneously.

04 Tendon Rehab Framework

Posterior tibial tendinopathy. The gating section.

Read this before any other section. Your right tendon is the gating factor for everything else in this plan.

⚠ Step Zero

If you have not seen a sports-focused PT in the last 3 months, see one before starting this plan. They will confirm the diagnosis, assess single-leg heel-raise capacity, identify upstream contributors (hip control, big toe mobility, foot intrinsics), and determine if you should use an orthotic or rocker shoe. Nothing here substitutes for that assessment.

How tendons heal: the load–pain–response framework

The Cook & Purdam continuum model places tendons on a spectrum: normal → reactive → disrepair → degenerative. Tendons heal by being loaded in a way just below the threshold that worsens symptoms, repeatedly, over months. They do not heal by being rested.

The 24-Hour Rule

The standard for tendinopathy rehab in sports medicine:

  • Symptoms during loading at ≤3/10 pain are acceptable
  • Symptoms within 24 hours after loading should return to baseline (your starting morning pain)
  • Morning pain higher than baseline the next day → that session was too much; back off 20–30% next time
  • Morning pain elevated for 24+ hours → drop further, reassess

You're aiming for: discomfort during loading, no flare in the 24 hours after.

The four phases of tendon loading

Phase A — Isometric calming (Wks 1–3 if symptomatic)

If your tendon is currently painful with daily activity, start here. Isometric loading reduces pain in many tendinopathies.

  • Heavy isometric calf hold, knee straight: 5 × 30–45 sec at 80%+ effort, 1–2× daily
  • Heavy isometric calf hold, knee bent (soleus focus): same dosing
  • Resisted inversion isometric (band, foot turned inward): 5 × 30 sec

Phase B — Heavy Slow Resistance (Wks 4–14)

The workhorse phase. Tempo: 3 sec up, 3 sec down. Frequency: 3×/week, 48+ hours between. Loading: Start at 12RM, do 3–4 sets of 12 reps. Every 2 weeks, increase load and reduce reps: 12RM → 10RM → 8RM → 6RM.

Core HSR exercises (all three each session):

  1. Bilateral heel raise on a step, slow lower into dorsiflexion, knee straight
  2. Bilateral heel raise on a step, knee bent ~30° (soleus emphasis)
  3. Single-leg heel raise on a step (once cleared) — most tib-post-specific

Adjunct (rotate 1–2 per session): Resisted ankle inversion (band), foot doming / short foot.

Phase C — Elastic / functional loading (Wks 12–18)

Once HSR at ~8RM is tolerated, add SSC work to prepare for impact loading.

  • Pogo hops 3 × 20, minimal contact time
  • Single-leg pogo hops 3 × 15 each
  • Skipping for height: A-skips, B-skips, 3 × 30 m
  • Low-amplitude lateral hops: 3 × 15 each direction

Phase D — Sport-specific maintenance (Wks 18+)

Once HSR is heavy (6RM) and elastic work is tolerated, explicit tendon work transitions to maintenance: usually 1×/week single-leg HSR + elastic drills in warmup. The bulk of the stimulus by this point is the running and strength work itself.

Running technique modifications

Three small cues that reduce tibialis posterior demand during the rebuild:

  1. Slightly wider stance width. If your feet cross over, widen by ~½ shoe. Imagine running on train tracks, not a tightrope.
  2. Cadence ~5% higher than default. If you're at 165 spm, aim for 170–175. Shorter, quicker steps reduce peak loads.
  3. Softer, smoother surfaces. Packed trail, treadmill, dirt road, track. Avoid steep cambered roads and gnarly off-camber terrain until late phases.
⚠ When to stop and consult your PT
  • Sharp pain (vs. dull ache) along the medial ankle/foot
  • Pain that makes you alter your gait
  • Swelling that doesn't resolve overnight
  • A new "popping" or "snapping" sensation
  • Loss of single-leg heel raise capacity

07 Supporting Programs

Strength, prehab, heat, fueling, monitoring.

The programs that sit under and around the running schedule. Every one of these is calibrated to the phase you're in.

A · Strength Training

Two 45–60 min sessions per week, separated by ≥48 hours. Always preceded by the dynamic warm-up and the prehab routine. The structure shifts across phases:

Block 1: Foundation (Wks 1–6) — Tissue Prep

Loads light to moderate. Groove patterns, tolerate basic loading.

  • Day A: Goblet squat 3×8 · Romanian deadlift 3×8 · Bulgarian split squat 3×8 each · Single-leg glute bridge 3×12 · Side plank 3×30 sec · HSR calf circuit
  • Day B: Reverse lunge 3×8 each · Step-up 3×8 each · Dead bug 3×10 each · Pallof press 3×10 each · SL balance 3×30 sec each · HSR calf circuit

Block 2: Max Strength (Wks 7–12) — Drive Force Production

Heavy compound lifts produce the running-economy benefits documented in Blagrove's work. Build to ~80–85% 1RM by Wk 12.

  • Day A: Back squat 4×5 · Romanian deadlift 4×5 · Bulgarian split squat (weighted) 3×6 each · Single-leg deadlift 3×6 each · Side plank with hip abduction 3×10 each · HSR calf
  • Day B: Front squat or trap-bar deadlift 4×5 · Weighted step-up 4×5 each · SL calf raise off step (weighted) 4×8 each · Cable pull-through 3×8 · Hollow body 3×30 sec · Foot intrinsic work 5 min
  • Plyometric primer (1×/wk): Pogo hops 3×20 · Box jumps (low, soft) 3×6 · Skipping for height 3×30 m

Block 3: Muscular Endurance (Wks 13–18) — Canyon-Specific

Reps go up, loads stay moderate-heavy, tempo controlled. Sessions feel like the strength version of an ultra: long, sustained, eventually grinding.

  • Day A — Heavy + ME circuit: Back squat 3×6 at 75% · Romanian deadlift 3×6 · ME circuit, 3 rounds: Walking lunge ×20 (loaded), Step-up ×12 each, SL calf raise ×15 each, Plank with reach ×10 each
  • Day B — Single-leg emphasis: Front squat 3×6 · Trap-bar deadlift 3×6 · ME circuit, 3 rounds: Bulgarian split squat ×12 each (weighted), Backward lunge ×12 each (specifically protects quads for downhill), SL Romanian deadlift ×10 each, SL pogo hops ×15 each

Block 4: Maintenance (Wks 19–24)

Reduce volume ~30%; preserve neural patterns. Drop heaviest sets, keep one heavy and one ME session weekly. By Wk 23, sessions are 30 min, light, movement-quality. Wk 24: no strength.

B · Dynamic Warm-up (5–10 min, every session)

Hip circles 10 each direction each leg · Leg swings (forward/back, side/side) · Walking lunges with rotation ×10 · Lateral lunges ×8 each · A-skips 30 m · B-skips 30 m · High knees 30 m · Butt kicks 30 m · Cariocas 30 m each direction · 4 × 20-sec strides (gradual acceleration, form focus).

C · Prehab / Mobility Routine (10–15 min, daily)

The ankle-foot-hip routine that protects the tibialis posterior. Non-negotiable.

  1. Ankle alphabet — write the alphabet with the foot, each leg
  2. Toe yoga — lift big toe with others down, then reverse · 3 × 10 each
  3. Foot doming / short foot — 5-sec hold × 10 reps each
  4. Marble pickups or towel scrunches — 1 min each foot
  5. Single-leg balance — 30 sec each, then 30 sec eyes-closed, progress to unstable surface
  6. Wall ankle dorsiflexion mobilization — knee toward wall over toe, ×10 each
  7. Calf stretch (knee straight) — 2 × 30 sec each
  8. Calf stretch (knee bent, soleus) — 2 × 30 sec each
  9. Hip flexor stretch (couch) — 2 × 30 sec each
  10. Glute med activation — banded clamshells 2 × 15 each, side-lying hip abduction 2 × 12 each

D · Heat Acclimation Protocol (Wk 17 onward)

Even 6–7 sessions produce meaningful adaptation; 10–14 sessions produce more. Begin Week 17, continue through race week.

Option 1 · Sauna (most studied)

4 sessions/week, immediately post-exercise. Traditional sauna 80–90°C / 175–195°F. Start 15 min, progress to 30 min by Wk 19. Target: pleasantly hot, sweating heavily, nose-breathable. If dizzy/nauseous, exit immediately. Hydrate 0.5 L (w/ electrolytes) before, 0.5–1 L after.

Option 2 · Hot bath

4 sessions/week post-exercise. ~40°C / 104°F. Submerge to shoulders, 30–40 min. Same hydration.

Option 3 · Outdoor heat run

1–2 runs/wk in midday heat, easy Z1–Z2, 40–60 min. More than this risks training stress + heat stress overlap → injury.

E · Trekking Pole Training

Reduce leg load on descents, improve climbing economy ~5%. Standard R2R2R equipment. Don't use them for the first time on race day. Introduce Wk 9. Use on ≥2 long runs/week starting Wk 13. Practice: quick setup (race day will be cold), stowing for runnable sections, plant timing on steep ups, defensive use on steep downs (in front, light touch, to brake/balance, not bear weight).

F · Fueling Strategy

Bonking and GI failure are the most common in-race issues after quad failure.

Training fueling targets (long runs >2 hr)

  • 60–80 g carbs/hr for 2–4 hr runs
  • 80–100 g carbs/hr for 4+ hr runs (use glucose+fructose blends)
  • 500–750 mL fluid/hr (more in heat)
  • 500–1,000 mg sodium/hr (more for salty sweaters)
  • Practice every long run from Wk 9. Train the gut like the legs.

Sweat rate test (Wks 14, 17, 20)

Empty bladder, weigh nude, run 60 min Z2, drink known fluid amount, weigh again. Sweat rate (L/hr) = body weight loss (kg) + fluid drunk (L) ÷ duration. Multiply by 1.1 for canyon heat estimate.

Carb-loading

3 days out: 8–10 g carbs/kg/day. Avoid fiber/fat overload day before; plain, familiar food.

G · Daily Monitoring

Track in a notebook or app:

  1. Morning resting HR — persistent +5 bpm = fatigue accumulating
  2. Subjective energy (1–10) — below 6 for 3+ days = back off
  3. Right ankle pain (0–10 at first weight-bearing step) — anything >2 trending up = scale back
  4. Sleep duration and quality
  5. Mood and motivation

The Four-Rule Traffic Light

If… Then
Tendon at baseline, energy good, sleep good Proceed with planned session
Tendon mildly above baseline (≤1/10), energy reduced Reduce planned session by 25%, drop intensity
Tendon symptomatic (2–4/10), or 2+ poor sleep nights Skip the hard session, replace with easy Z1 or rest
Tendon painful (>4/10) or any sharp pain Stop. Re-read Part 4. See PT before next session.
08 Taper, Race Day & Logistics

The final 48 hours, the pacing strategy, the gear.

Taper logic

~2 weeks of reduced volume (40–60% drop over taper), maintained or slightly increased relative intensity (keep doing strides and short hill reps), preserves and often improves fitness. Most performance gains from a good taper come from glycogen restoration, tendon repair, and small nervous-system adaptations.

Resist the urge to "test" your fitness with extra hard workouts in the final two weeks. Every extra hard effort risks injury or fatigue with zero upside.

Race-day pacing strategy

The pacing principle: conserve aggressively in segments 1 and 2; finish strong in segments 3 and 4.

Segment 1
SK Descent
~7 miles −4,800 ft 1:15–1:45 hr
  • Slowest sustainable downhill technique. Quad savings here = quad reserves at the end.
  • Heart rate well below AeT — should feel like a warmup.
  • Trekking poles deployed.
  • Fuel: 80 g carbs, 0.5 L fluid.
Segment 2
Box + N Rim Climb
~14 miles +5,800 ft 4–5 hr
  • Steady Z2 effort. Run flats and gentle ups; hike anything over ~8% grade.
  • Fuel: 80–100 g carbs/hr, 0.5–0.75 L/hr.
  • This is the long, sustained section where rhythm and patience matter. Don't surge.
  • At N Rim turnaround: top off fluids, eat real food, briefly stretch, restart.
Segment 3
N Rim Descent to River
~14 miles −5,800 ft 2:30–3:30 hr
  • Most dangerous segment. Quads now warmed up but not yet destroyed. Tempting to fly down.
  • Don't. Cruise. Use poles defensively. Land with bent knees, short stride.
  • Heart rate moderate (Z2). Effort relaxed even though pace is fast.
  • By now, eating may feel harder; force it.
Segment 4 · The Crux
SK Climb to S Rim
~7 miles +4,800 ft 2:30–3:30 hr
  • The crux. Hottest, hardest, most fatigued.
  • Hike all of it except briefly runnable flat sections at trailheads.
  • Pole work matters here. Plant aggressively, push with arms.
  • Fuel: continue 80 g/hr even if appetite is gone. Drop from gels to whatever you can stomach — fruit, soda, salted nuts, real food.
  • Heat: dunk shirt and hat at every water source. Eat ice if available. Slow down before overheating, not after.
  • Mantra: short steps, steady breathing, don't stop unless you need to.

Heat management

  • Start before sunrise — ideally 3–5 AM if temps will exceed 80°F at the bottom by midday
  • Goal: be on the climb up SK before noon if possible
  • Carry a buff or light cap that can be soaked
  • Dunk every shirt/hat opportunity in every water source
  • Chills, stop sweating, or nausea → heat illness warning, stop and cool down

Mandatory gear

  • Pack with 1.5–2 L hydration capacity
  • 2× soft flasks (refill flexibility)
  • Water filter or purification tablets (canyon water system unreliable; tap closures happen mid-day)
  • Trekking poles
  • Headlamp + spare batteries
  • Light wind shell (for rim cold)
  • Light layer (rims are 20–30°F cooler than the bottom)
  • Trail running gaiters (sand and grit destroy feet)
  • Emergency blanket / bivy
  • First-aid kit (blister supplies, anti-chafe, ibuprofen)
  • Printed trail map (no cell service)
  • 2,500+ calories of food
  • 1,500+ mg sodium beyond what's in gels/drink mix
  • ID, parking pass, emergency contact card

The final 48 hours

Friday (race−1)

  • Travel to South Rim, arrive by early afternoon
  • Drive the trailhead access; identify parking
  • Walk 20–30 min easy at the rim to acclimate to altitude
  • Eat dinner by 6 PM — familiar, moderate-carb, low-fat, low-fiber
  • Lay out all gear, pack, recheck list twice
  • Bed by 8 PM. Alarm 2:30–3 AM.

Race morning

  • Wake 90+ min before start
  • Eat 100–150 g carbs (oatmeal, banana, toast, sports drink)
  • Coffee if normal for you
  • Don't try anything new
  • At trailhead: 30 g carb gel + 250 mL water 15 min before starting
  • Cold rim — warm layer on, knowing you'll strip in the first mile

The day after

  • Eat. Sleep. Walk only.
  • Foam-roll lightly.
  • Within a week: 30-min easy walk, then a 20-min jog if pain-free.
  • Do not return to structured training for at least 2 weeks. The eccentric damage is bigger than you think.
